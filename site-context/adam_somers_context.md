### 6. Universal Audio — Senior Software Engineer (~2010–2012)

**The company:** Universal Audio is a household name in professional studio technology — they make audio interfaces, analog hardware emulations, and the UAD plugin platform used by musicians and producers worldwide. Based in the Santa Cruz mountains.

**Why it mattered to Adam:** Landing at UA was his first "dream job." He was at exactly the company he wanted to work at, surrounded by brilliant minds who were experts at the thing he wanted to be better at. It was incredibly humbling and satisfying — driving up over the Santa Cruz mountains in the early mornings, above the sea of clouds settled over the redwood forest, being part of a community that was as obsessed with surfing and the outdoors as it was with being the world's best audio company.

**The mission — "Adam '64-bit' Somers":**
Adam was hired specifically to address a core problem: Apple had deprecated the 32-bit kernel, putting a time bomb on any drivers and client apps developed without 64-bit support. By 2011, UA was still building on Metrowerks CodeWarrior, using QuickDraw APIs from the age of macOS 9. They were using CVS for revision control and lacked any automated testing.

Adam's role was to design, execute, and lead the migration to modern macOS APIs and development workflows. With **60 plugin products** in the portfolio, it was a truly daunting task that none of the seasoned developers wanted to take on — so they hired Adam. Because the codebase was cross-platform (Windows and macOS), he had to rebuild the shared UI layer and guarantee parity of audio and graphical experience. The update was a minor version bump (6.3 to 6.4) with the requirement that **users experience no discontinuity in their workflows whatsoever.**

**How he did it:**
- Meticulously planned the migration using estimation skills learned at Art & Logic
- Built an automated system for measuring audio and visual parity between builds
- Recruited a group of more senior peers and a small army of interns from a local community college to take tasks from the plan
- Completed the mission over nearly two years

**The outcome:** Adam earned the nickname **"Adam '64-bit' Somers"** and was awarded the **"Rockstar" designation** at the company's annual all-hands. This project cemented his identity as a confident, independent senior engineer capable of leading large-scale technical migrations across cross-platform codebases.

**Technology areas:** C/C++, cross-platform development (macOS/Windows), kernel extensions, hardware interfacing, UI frameworks, automated testing, plugin architectures

### Lead Skill — AI-Augmented Management Operating System
**Repo:** [github.com/AdamSomers/lead-skill](https://github.com/AdamSomers/lead-skill) (1 star)
**What it is:** A Claude Code skill that turns AI into a persistent, file-backed management operating system for engineering managers and leaders. Not a chatbot — a living system where management context accumulates and compounds over time.

**How it works:**
- Installs as a set of slash commands in Claude Code (`/lead-sync`, `/lead-prep`, `/lead-decide`, `/lead-draft`, `/lead-reflect`, `/lead-review`, etc.)
- Stores all context in structured markdown files: team rosters, priorities, org context, decision logs, 1:1 notes, performance reviews, daily journal entries
- Each command reads what it needs from files, does its work, and writes back — context persists and compounds across sessions
- Includes a library of leadership frameworks (Grove, Scott, Brooks, etc.)

**Key workflows:**
- Daily: sync → prep for meeting → meeting → debrief → repeat
- Decision: structured multi-perspective analysis → draft communication
- Weekly: reflect on patterns → recalibrate priorities
- Review cycle: generates performance feedback drafts from accumulated 1:1 notes and observations

**Why it matters:** This is the most direct expression of Adam's "AI as second mind" philosophy. It's built from years of actual management experience and encodes the patterns, frameworks, and workflows that real engineering managers need. The insight is that management context is valuable but perishable — and AI can help preserve and leverage it.

**"Ground this" — a concrete example:**
Adam runs `/lead-debrief` after a 1:1 and adds "ground this" to the prompt. The AI relates his notes to an appropriate leadership framework. Recently, a report who is a manager brought a tricky situation: a senior engineer didn't believe in the vision of the project he was assigned to tech lead. After debriefing, the system surfaced an applied example of Situational Leadership that was directly relevant — reminding Adam of a diagnosing technique he'd learned years ago. That framework proved useful in his next meeting with the report. The AI didn't solve the problem — it reminded Adam of a tool he already knew but wasn't reaching for in the moment.

**Vision:** Adam is interested in sharing Lead Skill more broadly after further iterations and personal use. It's currently shaped to his own workflow but the underlying pattern — persistent context + leadership frameworks + structured workflows — is applicable to any engineering manager.

**Built with:** Shell scripts, Claude Code commands, markdown templates. Developed entirely with Claude Code. Contributors: Adam Somers + Claude.

### Fitness AI — Personalized AI Fitness Trainer
**Repo:** [github.com/AdamSomers/fitness-app](https://github.com/AdamSomers/fitness-app)
**What it is:** A personalized AI fitness trainer that generates workouts tailored to your goals, equipment, and training history. Built with Streamlit and powered by Claude.

**The problem it solves:** Generic chatbots forget your equipment, lose track of your progress, and don't know what day it is. Fitness AI fixes this with persistent context — your goals, equipment, injuries, preferences, and full training history are always available to the AI when generating workouts.

**How it works:**
1. **Profile setup** — Enter fitness goals, available equipment, injuries, training preferences. This context accompanies every AI request.
2. **Training plans** — Create plans (e.g., "Strength Training" with a Lower/Upper/Full Body rotation). Multiple active plans supported.
3. **AI workout generation** — Select a plan, optionally add context ("feeling tired", "want to focus on back"), and Claude generates a workout informed by your profile, plan, and recent history. Review, edit, and queue for logging.
4. **Freeform workout logging** — After training, enter what you actually did in natural language (e.g., "DL 3x5 @ 155, skipped lunges") and the AI parses it into structured exercise entries.
5. **History & review** — Browse past workouts with filtering. Import historical training data from text.

**Developer features:**
- Data export/import (JSON) for backup and migration
- Plan rollout simulation — preview how a plan evolves over time by generating a sequence of workouts as if each were completed, validating variety and progression
- Test suite with pytest

**Tech stack:** Python, Streamlit, Claude API (Anthropic SDK), SQLite, uv package manager

**Architecture:**
```
fitness-app/
├── app.py                 # Main Streamlit entry point
├── pages/                 # Streamlit pages (Generate, Log, History, Profile, Plans, Dev Tools)
├── services/              # Business logic
├── database/              # SQLite repositories
├── llm/                   # Claude API integration
├── models/                # Domain models
└── tests/                 # Test suite
```

**Why it matters:** This is the same "AI as persistent context" philosophy as Lead Skill, applied to a completely different domain. The insight is the same: AI gets dramatically more useful when it has structured, accumulated context rather than starting from zero each conversation. It also demonstrates Adam building a full-stack application end-to-end — product design, backend, AI integration, and UX — as a side project.

**Personal use:** Adam actively uses Fitness AI for his own training. He's a runner who trains for ultra marathons and does strength training at home — making the app a real tool for a demanding discipline, not a demo project.

### The "AI as Second Mind" Philosophy

Across all three projects, Adam is exploring a consistent thesis: AI works best not as a replacement for human thinking, but as an augmentation layer that helps people organize, decide, and communicate. The key technical insight shared by Lead Skill and Fitness AI is that **AI gets dramatically more useful with persistent, structured context** — not one-off conversations but accumulated state that compounds over time. This shows up as:

- **Lead Skill:** AI that helps managers think more clearly about their teams and priorities, backed by persistent files that accumulate context across sessions
- **Fitness AI:** AI that generates better workouts because it knows your equipment, injuries, goals, and full training history — solving the "amnesia" problem of generic chatbots
- **Fk2:** AI as a collaborator in building creative tools (Claude as co-developer on a C++ audio project)

**In Adam's own words:** The "second mind" takes all the notes, reflections, and journal entries that would normally just sit there — hard to scroll through, hard to find the important parts — and processes them into meaningful takeaways and action items. It helps reflect at the end of each day and think about the most important things to do. It also grounds thinking in literature and frameworks that have resonated in the past but that you don't tend to reach for on a day-to-day basis. In this sense, it provides a lens through which to understand what you're thinking about from different perspectives.

**The key distinction:** Most people use AI as ask-a-question, get-an-answer. Adam is building toward AI that knows your ongoing context — your team, your goals, your training history, your past decisions — and uses that context to make your own thinking more durable, retrievable, and grounded. The AI doesn't think for you; it makes sure your best thinking doesn't get lost.

**All three projects were built entirely with Claude Code** — making Adam both a practitioner of AI-augmented development and a designer of AI-augmented tools.

## Leadership Philosophy

Adam's management approach is grounded in a few deeply held principles, expressed in his own words:

### The Three-Legged Stool
A healthy career sits on three legs: **what you enjoy, what you're best at, and what the company needs.** As a leader, it's Adam's job to provide the business with what it needs. As a human being, he takes responsibility for providing his employees with a good experience and a growth opportunity. When all three align, people do their best work.

### Growth by Stretching
Inherited from his Jaunt CEO's philosophy — *"the only way to grow is to stretch beyond where you're comfortable"* — Adam actively applies this to people who have potential. This means assigning people into unexpected growth areas: putting a mobile developer on an ML evaluation system, or giving a quality engineer ownership of data schemas for eval datasets. He sees growth opportunities that people might not be considering for themselves.

### How Adam Leads Day-to-Day
1. **Synthesizing company needs into deliverables** the team can own
2. **Casting a vision** for what the world looks like in 2-3 years after the team has done its part
3. **Building a 6-12 month roadmap** collaboratively with the team — achievable and burnout-proof. Managing stakeholder expectations is the lynchpin of success here.
4. **Assigning ownership based on skills, interests, and growth potential** — matching people to work where they can both contribute and stretch
5. **Keeping an open line of communication with all people in his rollup.** Adam meets regularly with everyone in his organization, not just direct reports. This is a significant time investment that pays dividends in strong relationships and team identity — evidenced by very low attrition on his teams. These meetings are also his opportunity to directly coach or play an advisory role to managers under him whom he is teaching to coach better.

### Shared Identity Across Sub-Teams
When scaling a team (as Adam did from 5 to 20+ at Meta), the hardest parts are expanding the scope and core remit, and building trust with leadership and partners on the value proposition of a new area of investment. A key requirement is creating a sense of shared identity across sub-teams, so that members don't feel like they're working in the same silo just further down the org chart. Each person on the team needs to understand the enduring value they are collectively bringing to customers, and each person should know how their contribution — whether a large area of technical ownership or a focused task — ladders up to that value.

### The Hardest Parts of Scaling
At Meta, the challenge wasn't just hiring — it was building conviction with leadership and partners that a small audio infrastructure team deserved to become a larger investment area. This required Adam to evangelize the value proposition across disparate organizations, often to stakeholders with competing priorities, before the team had the resources to fully deliver on the vision.

### How the Arts Background Informs Leadership

At CalArts, technology was utilitarian toward the creation of art — yet off-the-shelf tools didn't exist for Adam's artistic goals, so he found himself developing technology as a means to an artistic end. Toolmaking became a creative act in and of itself, which led to a sense of craft in the art of computer programming. Adam would read texts by John Cage on the same day as texts by Kernighan and Ritchie — art and technology were not separate, they were two parts of a whole.

**Engineering as a creative act:** Engineers in Silicon Valley may or may not think of themselves as artists, but every time you press compile in your IDE or push a PR to main, you are engaging in an act of creativity. In his role as manager, Adam stays tuned in to the ups and downs of what a creative person goes through. Some days the flow is just not there, which is totally fine. Other days a tempest is being unleashed, and it must be harnessed in a way that is as gratifying for the individual as it is valuable for the company.

**Seeing people as people:** CalArts contained a kaleidoscopic array of people — from the most sober, analytical mathematician to the most eccentric performance artist. Adam interacted with types of people he couldn't have imagined. The tech world is often stereotyped as a monoculture of CS graduates, but Adam interacts with people as people, and finds the same array of idiosyncrasies in each colleague as he did in art school.
